§ 16-14.1 Intent.
   The purpose of this article is to establish minimum life safety design criteria for enhanced hurricane protection areas within high occupancy State- or county-owned buildings permitted to be occupied during hurricanes of up to Saffir Simpson Category 3.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.1) (Added by Ord. 12-34, Am. Ords. 20-7, 24-15)
§ 16-14.2 Scope.
   This article applies to Risk Category Ill and IV buildings defined by International Building Code Table 1604.5, for the following specific occupancies:
(1)   Enclosed and partially enclosed (covered) structures whose primary occupancy is public assembly with an occupant load greater than 300;
(2)   Health care facilities with an occupant load of 50 or more resident patients, but not having surgery or emergency treatment facilities;
(3)   Any other State- or county-owned building with an occupant load greater than 5,000 people; and
(4)   Hospitals and other health care facilities having surgery or emergency treatment facilities.
Exception: Facilities located within Flood Zone V and Flood Zone A that are designated by the owner to be evacuated during hurricane warnings declared by the National Weather Service, are not subject to these requirements.
(Added by Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-14.3 Site criteria.
(a)   Comply with ASCE 24-14, Flood Resistant Design and Construction, based on provisions for Risk Category III.
(1)   Floor slab on grade must be 1.5 feet above the base flood elevation of the county’s flood hazard map, or a higher elevation as determined by a modeling methodology that predicts the maximum envelope and depth of inundation including the combined effects of storm surge and wave actions with respect to a Category 3 hurricane, nor less than the flood elevation associated with a 500-year mean recurrence interval.
(2)   Locate outside of V and Coastal A flood zones unless justified by site-specific analysis or designed for vertical evacuation in accordance with a method approved by the building official. When a building within a V or Coastal A flood zone is approved, the bottom of the lowest structural framing member of any elevated first floor space must be 2 feet above the base flood elevation of the county’s flood hazard map, or at higher elevation as determined by a modeling methodology that predicts the maximum envelope and depth of inundation including the combined effects of storm surge and wave actions with respect to a Category 3 hurricane, nor less than the flood elevation associated with a 500-year mean recurrence interval.
(b)   Emergency vehicle access. Provide at least one route for emergency vehicle access. The portion of the emergency route within the site must be above the 100-year flood elevation.
(c)   Landscaping and utility laydown impact hazards. Landscaping around the building must be designed to provide standoff separation sufficient to maintain emergency vehicle access in the event of mature tree blowdown. Trees must not interfere with the functioning of overhead or underground utility lines, nor cause laydown or falling impact hazard to the building envelope or utility lines.
(d)   Adjacent buildings. The building must not be located within 1,000 feet of any hazardous material facilities defined by International Building Code Table 1604.5. Unanchored light-framed portable structures must not be permitted within 300 feet of the building, unless the windborne debris hazard of the portable structure uplift is mitigated.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.2) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-14.4 Enhanced hurricane protection area program requirements.
(a)   Applicable net area. At least 50 percent of the net square feet of a facility must be constructed to qualify as an enhanced hurricane protection area. The net floor area is determined by subtracting from the gross square feet the floor area of excluded spaces, exterior walls, columns, fixed or movable objects, equipment, or other features that under probable conditions cannot be removed or stored during use as a storm shelter.
(b)   Excluded spaces. Spaces such as mechanical rooms, electrical rooms, storage rooms, attic and crawl spaces, must not be considered as net floor area permitted to be occupied during a hurricane.
(c)   Occupancy capacity. The occupancy capacity is determined by dividing the net area of the enhanced hurricane protection area by 15 square feet net floor area per person.
(d)   Toilets and hand washing facilities. Toilet and hand washing facilities must be located and accessible from within the perimeter of the enhanced hurricane protection area.
(e)   Accessibility. Where the refuge occupancy accommodates more than 50 persons, provide an ADA-accessible route to a shelter area at each facility with a minimum of one wheelchair space for every 200 enhanced hurricane protection area occupants determined in accordance with § 16-14.4(c).
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.3) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-14.5 Design wind, rain, and impact loads.
(a)   Structural design criteria. The building main wind force resisting system and structural components must be designed per ASCE 7 for a 145 mph minimum peak 3-second gust ultimate design wind speed.
Topographic and directionality factors must be the site-specific values determined per State Building Code Appendix W. Design for interior pressure must be based on the largest opening in any exterior facade or roof surface.
(b)   Windborne debris missile impact for building enclosure elements. Exterior glazing and glazed openings, louvers, roof openings and doors must be provided with windborne debris impact resistance or protection systems conforming to ASTM E1996-14 Level D, i.e., 9 lb. 2 X 4@ 50 fps (34 mph).
(c)   Cyclic pressure loading of impact resistive glazing or windborne impact protective systems. Resistance to the calculated maximum inward and outward pressure must be designed to conform to ASTM E1996-14.
(d)   Windows. All unprotected window assemblies and their anchoring systems must be designed and installed to meet the wind load and missile impact criteria of this section.
(e)   Window protective systems. Windows may be provided with permanent or deployable protective systems; provided that the protective system is designed and installed to meet the wind load and missile impact criteria and completely covers the window assembly and anchoring system.
(f)   All exterior and interior doors subject to possible wind exposure or missile impact must have doors, frames, anchoring devices, and vision panels designed and installed to resist the wind load and missile impact criteria or such doors, frames, anchoring devices, and vision panels must be provided with impact protective systems designed and installed to resist the wind load and missile impact criteria of this section.
(g)   Exterior envelope. The building enclosure, including walls, roofs, glazed openings, louvers, and doors, must not be perforated or penetrated by windborne debris, as determined by compliance with ASTM E1996-14 Level D.
(h)   Parapets. Parapets must satisfy the wind load and missile impact criteria of the exterior envelope.
(i)   Roofs.
(1)   Roof openings. Roof openings (e.g., HVAC fans, ducts, skylights) must be provided with protection for the wind load and missile impact criteria of §§ 16-14.5(b) and 16-14.5(c).
(2)   High wind roof coverings. Roof coverings must be specified and designed according to the latest ASTM Standards for high wind uplift forces or International Building Code Section 1507, whichever is greater.
(3)   Roof drainage. Roofs must have adequate slope, drains, and overflow drains or scuppers sized to accommodate 100-year hourly rainfall rates in accordance with International Building Code Section 1611.1 and Figure 1611.1 but not less than 2 inches per hour for 6 continuous hours.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.4) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-14.6 Ventilation.
(a)   Mechanical ventilation. Mechanical ventilation as required in accordance with the International Mechanical Code. Air intakes and exhausts must be designed and installed to meet the wind load and missile impact criteria of this article.
(b)   HVAC equipment anchorage. HVAC equipment mounted on roofs and anchoring systems must be designed and installed to meet the wind load criteria. Roof openings for roof-mounted HVAC equipment must have a 12-inch-high curb designed to prevent the entry of rain water.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.5) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15, 25-11)
§ 16-14.7 Standby electrical system capability.
(a)   Provide a standby emergency electrical power system per International Building Code Chapter 27 and NFPA 70 Article 700 Emergency Systems and Article 701 Legally Required Standby Systems that has the capability of being connected to an emergency generator or other temporary power source. The emergency system capabilities must include:
(1)   An emergency lighting system;
(2)   Illuminated exit signs;
(3)   Fire protection systems, fire alarm systems, and fire sprinkler systems; and
(4)   Minimum mechanical ventilation for health/safety purposes.
(b)   Emergency generator. When emergency generators are pre-installed, the facility housing the generator, permanent or portable, must be an enclosed area designed to protect the generators from wind and missile impact.
Generators hardened by the manufacturer to withstand the area’s design wind and missile impact criteria must be exempt from the enclosed area criteria requirement.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.6) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-14.8 Quality assurance.
(a)   Information on construction documents. Construction documents must include design criteria, the occupancy capacity of the enhanced hurricane protective area, and Project Specifications must include opening protection devices. Floor plans must indicate all enhanced hurricane protection area portions of the facility and exiting routes therefrom. The latitude and longitude coordinates of the building must be recorded on the construction documents.
(b)   Special inspection. In addition to the requirements of International Building Code Chapter 17, special inspections must include at least the following systems and components:
(1)   Roof cladding and roof framing connections;
(2)   Wall connections to roof and floor diaphragms and framing;
(3)   Roof and floor diaphragm systems, including collectors, drag struts, and boundary elements;
(4)   Vertical windforce-resisting systems, including braced frames, moment frames, and shear walls;
(5)   Windforce-resisting system connections to the foundation; and
(6)   Fabrication and installation of systems or components required to meet the impact-resistance requirements of the Hawaii State Building Code Section 1609.1.2.
Exception: Fabrication of manufactured systems or components that have a label indicating compliance with the wind-load and impact-resistance requirements of this code.
(c)   Quality assurance plan. A construction quality assurance program must be included in the construction documents and must include:
(1)   The materials, systems, components, and work required to have special inspection or testing by the building official or by the registered design professional responsible for each portion of the work;
(2)   The type and extent of each special inspection;
(3)   The type and extent of each test;
(4)   Additional requirements for special inspection or testing for seismic or wind resistance; and
(5)   For each type of special inspection, identification as to whether it is continuous special inspection or periodic special inspection.
(d)   Peer review. Construction documents must be independently reviewed by a Hawaii-licensed structural engineer. A written opinion report of compliance must be submitted to the Hawaii Emergency Management Agency, the building official, and the owner.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.7) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-14.9 Compliance re-certification if altered, deteriorated, or damaged.
   Alterations must be reviewed by a Hawaii-licensed structural engineer to determine whether any alterations would cause a violation of this section. Deterioration or damage to any component of the building requires an evaluation by a Hawaii-licensed structural engineer to determine repairs necessary to maintain compliance with this section.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 14, § 16-14.9) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
