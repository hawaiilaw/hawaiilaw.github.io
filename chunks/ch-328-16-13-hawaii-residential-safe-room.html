§ 16-13.1 Performance-based design criteria.
   The residential safe room must meet the performance-based design criteria’s minimum performance specifications of this article.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.1) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.2 Intent and scope.
   The intent of the residential safe room is to temporarily provide an enhanced protection area, fully enclosed within a dwelling or within an accessory structure to a residence, which is designed and constructed to withstand the wind pressures, windborne debris impacts, and other requirements of this section.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.2) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.3 Alternative standards.
(a)   Manufactured safe room designs subject to approval. A manufactured safe room or safe room kit may be substituted if documentation is submitted and approved by the building official. The safe room must be engineered, tested, and manufactured to meet or exceed the criteria of this section.
(b)   FEMA in-residence shelter designs permitted. It is permissible to build FEMA In Residence Shelters of up to 64 square feet of floor area with walls up to 8 feet long that are built in accordance with construction details of FEMA 320.
(c)   In addition to other applicable requirements in this code, storm shelters must be constructed in accordance with ICC-500 and this article and Article 14.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.3) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.4 Site criteria.
   Residential safe rooms must not be constructed within areas subject to stream flooding, coastal flooding, or dam failure inundation within any of the following areas:
(a)   FEMA Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHA) subject to rainfall runoff or stream or flash flooding;
(b)   Coastal zones “V” or “A” identified in the Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM) issued by FEMA for floodplain management purposes, in which the flood hazard are tides, storm surge, waves, tsunamis, or a combination of these hazards; and
(c)   Areas subject to dam failure inundation as determined by the State department of land and natural resources.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.4) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.5 Size of safe room.
   The safe room must be designed to provide a minimum of 7 square feet per person for one- and two-family dwellings and 10 square feet per person for other types of dwellings, based on usable floor area. Usable floor area is defined as the protected occupant area between the safe room walls at the level of fixed seating, where fixed seating exists, minus the area of sanitary facilities or other items that impede usage of the safe room area. Occupant load for a dwelling must not be less than the number of bedrooms plus one. The room does not need to exceed 120 square feet.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.5) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.6 Provisions for exiting.
   The safe room must be equipped with an impact protected, inward-swinging interior door or exterior door suitable for a means of exiting in an emergency.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.6) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.7 Design for dead, live, wind, rain, and impact loads.
(a)   Structural integrity criteria.
(1)   The residential safe room must be built with a complete structural system and a complete load path for vertical and lateral loads caused by gravity and wind.
(2)   The ceiling structure and wall must be capable of supporting a superimposed debris dead load of the full weight of any building floors and roof above, but not less than 60 psf.
(3)   The residential safe room enclosure must be capable of simultaneously resisting lateral and uplift with pressures corresponding to a 145 mph 3-second peak gust ultimate design wind speed, determined in accordance with ASCE-7, Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures. The site exposure factor must be based on exposure C or the exposure zone shown in State Building Code Appendix W Figure 1609.4, whichever is greater. The values for the gust factor and the directionality factor must be taken as 0.85. Topographic wind amplification caused by mountainous terrain must be considered in accordance with the building code. Internal pressure must be determined in accordance with ASCE-7.
(b)   Windborne debris impact protection of building enclosure elements. The exterior walls, vents, openings, fixed or operable windows, and entry doors into the safe room, must meet or exceed Level D requirements of ASTM E 1996 (Table 422.5-1), or be an approved assembly listed in this article. The interior walls, doors, openings, and ceilings into the safe room must meet or exceed Level C requirements of ASTM E 1996 (Table 422.5-1), or be an approved assembly listed in State Building Code Appendix U Section 429.5.2. Any exterior wall penetrations greater than 36 square inches must be considered an opening.
(c)   Debris impact and cyclic pressure loading of glazing and protective systems. Impact protective systems must meet the ASTM E 1996 cyclic pressure requirement for the loading given in Table 16.13.1.

§ 16-13.8 Ventilation.
   The residential safe room must be naturally ventilated to allow the enclosure to have approximately one air change every two hours. This requirement may be satisfied by a total of 12 square inches of venting per occupant. There must be at least two ventilation sources. The requirements for natural and mechanical must be in accordance with Chapter 16A.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.8) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.9 Construction documents.
   Construction documents for the residential safe room must be directly prepared by a Hawaii-licensed structural engineer.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.10) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
§ 16-13.10 Special inspection.
   The construction or installation of the residential safe room must be verified for conformance to the drawings in accordance with the appropriate requirements of International Building Code Chapter 17.
(1990 Code, Ch. 16, Art. 13, § 16-13.12) (Added by Ord. 12-34; Am. Ord. 24-15)
